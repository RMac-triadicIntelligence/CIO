Collateralized Insurance Obligations (CIOs)
A Balanced Framework for Financing Better Health Outcomes and Sustainable Coverage

Primary Author
Rusty Williams McMurray

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
Health systems face a persistent structural challenge: expanding access to care and improving health outcomes while controlling long‑term cost growth. Traditional fee‑for‑service reimbursement rewards utilization rather than prevention, while existing value‑based programs have demonstrated meaningful savings but lack durable financing mechanisms capable of scaling early and longitudinal care.

This white paper presents Collateralized Insurance Obligations (CIOs), a constrained financial framework designed to align capital, care delivery, and public oversight around measurable improvements in health outcomes and total cost of care (TCOC). CIOs are non‑leveraged, non‑synthetic instruments whose returns derive solely from verified surplus generated when defined patient populations achieve better outcomes at lower cost relative to locked, risk‑adjusted baselines. 

Financial participation is capped, audit‑gated, and subordinate to patient continuity and system stability.
The CIO model does not replace insurance, public authority, or value‑based care programs. Instead, it provides a financing layer that enables those systems to invest earlier, act consistently, and sustain improvements over time.

1. THE PROBLEM CIOs ADDRESS
Healthcare spending continues to rise faster than wages, GDP, and public revenues. Policymakers and regulators are repeatedly forced to choose between coverage expansion, provider stability, and fiscal restraint. Short budget cycles and fragmented incentives often delay preventive investments until after avoidable crises occur.
CMS value‑based programs, including the Medicare Shared Savings Program, have demonstrated that aligned incentives can reduce cost growth while maintaining or improving quality. In recent performance years, these programs generated billions in net savings. However, these savings remain programmatic; they do not create investable structures that allow capital to finance the care improvements that produce them.
CIOs are designed to address this gap by transforming verified cost containment and outcome improvements into a constrained, auditable financing mechanism without introducing leverage, speculation, or patient risk.

2. WHAT IS A COLLATERALIZED INSURANCE OBLIGATION (CIO)?
A Collateralized Insurance Obligation is a financial instrument representing a contractual claim on verified surplus generated when a defined patient cohort outperforms pre‑established cost and quality benchmarks. Unlike prior structured finance instruments, CIOs are intentionally limited in scope and behavior.
CIOs use no external leverage, prohibit synthetic replication, and permit distributions only after independent audit verification. Returns are capped, reserves are mandatory, and instruments are time‑settled rather than actively traded. Each patient is treated as an irreducible unit, and CIOs scale only through population size and time, not financial engineering.

3. STRUCTURAL ARCHITECTURE
Patient cohorts are defined using transparent, risk‑adjusted criteria such as clinical complexity, utilization history, and social risk factors. Pool selection is performed independently to prevent adverse selection and preserve equity.
Operators are evaluated on governance capacity, care coordination infrastructure, and execution history rather than patient selection. Their obligations emphasize earlier, more consistent care rather than service reduction.
CIOs are issued through Special Purpose Vehicles (SPVs) that isolate surplus claims from operating entities. SPVs hold contractual rights only to verified surplus, limiting contagion risk and preserving regulatory clarity.

4. CAPITAL FLOWS AND INCENTIVES
Capital raised through CIO issuance funds preventive and longitudinal interventions including care coordination, chronic disease management, adherence support, and approved social care services.
Surplus is distributed in a fixed order. Mandatory reserves are funded first to absorb shocks. Capped investor returns follow only after audit verification. Remaining surplus is reinvested in care delivery or patient‑benefit initiatives. Investor participation never supersedes patient access or continuity.

5. PROJECTED SAVINGS AND SYSTEM IMPACT
Empirical evidence from mature value‑based care programs consistently demonstrates per‑beneficiary annual savings in the range of $200–$300 while maintaining quality standards. CIOs are designed to amplify these known savings mechanisms by enabling earlier intervention and multi‑year care continuity.
Conservative projections suggest annual savings of $150–$300 for low‑risk cohorts, $400–$900 for medium‑risk chronic populations, and $1,500–$4,000 for high‑risk complex patients over multi‑year horizons. In an illustrative scenario covering five million beneficiaries, blended savings of approximately $500 per person annually would yield roughly $2.5 billion in gross annual savings.

6. HUMAN OUTCOME IMPACT
Cost containment alone does not justify healthcare reform. Public support depends on improvements people experience directly. CIO‑supported systems are designed to reduce avoidable hospital admissions, enable earlier and more consistent care, and preserve continuity during economic or system stress.
For patients, this translates into fewer medical crises, fewer late‑stage interventions, and more predictable care. For families, it reduces financial volatility, missed workdays, and caregiver strain. For communities, it eases pressure on emergency departments and frontline clinicians, improving system stability.

7. SAFEGUARDS AND FAILURE CONTAINMENT
CIOs are designed to fail safely rather than catastrophically. If outcomes deteriorate or costs rise, investor distributions pause automatically, reserves maintain care delivery, and corrective adjustments are implemented. Pools continue operating without patient displacement or abrupt withdrawal of services.

8. ETHICAL AND REGULATORY POSTURE
CIOs prohibit patient exclusion, preserve beneficiary rights, and maintain full HIPAA compliance. Independent audits, transparent reporting, and co‑regulation by health and financial authorities ensure public accountability. Patients are never tradable assets; only anonymized pool performance is referenced.

9. LIMITATIONS AND OPEN QUESTIONS
CIOs require robust data infrastructure, conservative actuarial standards, and clear regulatory authority. Pilots must precede scaling, and savings are expected to be incremental rather than immediate. No guarantees of outcomes or universal applicability are implied.

CONCLUSION
Collateralized Insurance Obligations provide a disciplined method for aligning capital with better health outcomes while preserving public trust and fiscal responsibility. By tying financial participation strictly to verified improvements and embedding safeguards that prioritize continuity and equity, CIOs offer a balanced pathway toward sustainable coverage expansion.
